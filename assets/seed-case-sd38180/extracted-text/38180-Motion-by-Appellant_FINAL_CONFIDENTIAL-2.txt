                            STANDARD OF REVIEW
      In the appeal concerning the interests of K.C.G., and the subsequent legal
matters involving M.P.G., Appellant vs. B.L.M. and C.T.A.M., Jasper County
Juvenile Office, Respondents, the standards of review that the Missouri Court of
Appeals, Southern District, should employ are delineated as follows:
1. DE NOVO REVIEW:
      For questions of jurisdiction and legal interpretation, which involve the
thorough examination of statutory provisions, case law, and constitutional
principles, a de novo standard of review is essential. When reviewing these
matters, the Appellate Court exercises an independent, non-deferential judgment.
This court must reassess the standard of review for legal issues presented without
presumption of correctness of the lower court’s ruling, ensuring that the legal
foundations dictating the outcome of the case are solidly in accordance with the
law and legal precedent.
2. SUBSTANTIAL EVIDENCE STANDARD:
      In reviewing the factual findings made by the trial Court, the standard of
review should shift to the substantial evidence test. This standard directs the
appellate court to affirm the lower court's determinations if the findings are
supported by substantial evidence on the record. Substantial evidence is such
relevant evidence that a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a
conclusion, even if it is possible to draw two inconsistent conclusions from the
evidence. (“Giha v. Garland, No. 15-73085 (9th Cir. 2021) :: Justia”) The
question is not whether the evidence could support a different conclusion but
whether it supports the conclusion that was reached by the trial Court.
3. CLEARLY ERRONEOUS STANDARD:
       When addressing the potential clear error in the trial court’s findings of fact,
it is warranted that such findings are left undisturbed unless the reviewing court, on
the entire evidence, is left with the definite and firm conviction that a mistake has
been made. This heightened degree of assurance in the trial court's factual accuracy
recognizes the trial judge’s role as the primary factfinder, having had the
opportunity to observe witness demeanor and to weigh the evidence presented
firsthand.
       The employment of these distinct standards of review acknowledges the dual
nature of appellate scrutiny, where legal issues are reviewed with precision and
independence, and factual determinations are accorded deference unless they fall
short of the evidentiary benchmark or are manifestly erroneous.

prejudiced the defense.

Counsel’s performance was deficient in several critical respects:

sufficiency of the evidence against the appellant and motions to suppress evidence

to parent.
· Inadequate communication: Counsel failed to maintain reasonable
communication with the appellant, depriving him of the opportunity to make
informed decisions and to contribute effectively to his own defense.
2. Prejudice to the Defense
The deficient performance of counsel resulted in substantial prejudice to the
defense. Critical evidence favorable to the appellant was not introduced,
cross-examination of the state's witnesses was insufficient, and potential defenses
were not raised, thereby denying the appellant the opportunity for a fair trial.
According to McKaskle v. Wiggins,
this failure undermines the “adversary process that our system counts on to

                            STANDARD OF REVIEW
      In the appeal concerning the interests of K.C.G., and the subsequent legal
matters involving M.P.G., Appellant vs. B.L.M. and C.T.A.M., Jasper County
Juvenile Office, Respondents, the standards of review that the Missouri Court of
Appeals, Southern District, should employ are delineated as follows:
1. DE NOVO REVIEW:
      For questions of jurisdiction and legal interpretation, which involve the
thorough examination of statutory provisions, case law, and constitutional
principles, a de novo standard of review is essential. When reviewing these
matters, the Appellate Court exercises an independent, non-deferential judgment.
This court must reassess the standard of review for legal issues presented without
presumption of correctness of the lower court’s ruling, ensuring that the legal
foundations dictating the outcome of the case are solidly in accordance with the
law and legal precedent.
2. SUBSTANTIAL EVIDENCE STANDARD:
      In reviewing the factual findings made by the trial Court, the standard of
review should shift to the substantial evidence test. This standard directs the
appellate court to affirm the lower court's determinations if the findings are
supported by substantial evidence on the record. Substantial evidence is such
relevant evidence that a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a
conclusion, even if it is possible to draw two inconsistent conclusions from the
evidence. (“Giha v. Garland, No. 15-73085 (9th Cir. 2021) :: Justia”) The
question is not whether the evidence could support a different conclusion but
whether it supports the conclusion that was reached by the trial Court.
3. CLEARLY ERRONEOUS STANDARD:
       When addressing the potential clear error in the trial court’s findings of fact,
it is warranted that such findings are left undisturbed unless the reviewing court, on
the entire evidence, is left with the definite and firm conviction that a mistake has
been made. This heightened degree of assurance in the trial court's factual accuracy
recognizes the trial judge’s role as the primary factfinder, having had the
opportunity to observe witness demeanor and to weigh the evidence presented
firsthand.
       The employment of these distinct standards of review acknowledges the dual
nature of appellate scrutiny, where legal issues are reviewed with precision and
independence, and factual determinations are accorded deference unless they fall
short of the evidentiary benchmark or are manifestly erroneous.
